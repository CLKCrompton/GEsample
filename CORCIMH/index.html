About


The main manuscript of the first part of the General e Grand Estoria is Mss 816, a codex called by the acronym A, which is kept in the National Library of Spain (BNE). The exact date of this copy is unknown, since it lacks a colophon, but it could be dated to 1275.

The text is a well-preserved parchment codex. It has 342 folios, arranged in 43 quaternions of 8 folios (except one of 6), with catchwords, and follows the Gregory’s rule, by which the flesh sides and the hair sides are respectively faced. It presents three cover pages at the beginning and at the end. The folios reach an approximate size of 410 x 292 mm. The foliation is modern, perhaps of the eighteenth century, and presents a couple of errors: after folio 25 the next one was ignored, which is now 25 bis; the numbering of folio 312 was omitted.

The text shows a very careful librarian gothic writing made by several hands, although it contains some errors and corrections. It presents a double column layout, with 41 lines each one. It has a discreet ornamentation in blue and red, colors also used for enlarged capital letters. The titles of the chapters appear in red.

Currently, the edition is an HTML-based critical edition, complete with notes and corresponding pictures of the manuscript. Forthcoming are many more aspects of the scholarly website such as a palaeographic edition of the manuscript and a critical edition of the text using the TEI, and a translation into English with notes from specialists in the field. HTML CRITICAL EDITION

  • This edition is a facsimile mark-up using HTML. The pages of the site are linked, each representing a page of the manuscript. Each page contains a picture of the page in the codex that it represents, and can be engaged with. Beside the picture is an HTML mark-up of the original text, including notes on the manuscript, as well as textual decisions that represent important aspects of the manuscript. These decisions are outlined below.

  • Italics represent letters that are different somehow in the manuscript. Either in the form of large, illuminated letters or redacted text.

  • Note that line endings are respected.

  • Superscript is used to denote letters that have been redacted in favour of a superscript apostrophe-like remark.

  • Blue text denotes titles.

  • Red Letters are Illuminated initials or text that is coloured to match the manuscript.

  • Pages are navigated by linked text in top right corner of the page

  • The hyphen indicates the word continues on the next line.


Legend

  • ¶ = Pilcrow marks the beginning of a new paragraph

  • [fol. 1r]= recto folio [each page on the right in a codex; in this case, number one]

  • [fol. 1v]= verso folio [each page on the left in a codex; in this case, number one]

  • [col. A]= denotes start of column [each page has two columns, in this case this is the first column, A]

Team


Francisco Peña Fernández (UBC Okanagan): is Associate Professor at the University of British Columbia. He earned his PhD in Hebrew Philology and Religious Studies from the Universidad Complutense de Madrid and his PhD in Medieval Spanish Literature from the University of California at Davis under the direction of Prof. Samuel Armistead. Dr. Peña’s work is wide-ranging and interdisciplinary in nature, involving Literary Studies, Biblical Studies, Religious Studies, and History of Ideas. His main fields of study include and combine the literary study of the Bible, the influence of the Bible in Medieval and Early Modern Spanish literature, and Jewish/Christian relations in Medieval Iberia. In addition to his two monographs, two critical editions and a number of peer-reviewed articles and book chapters, he has presented his research at international academic conferences, and is sought out as an invited lecturer and keynote speaker.

Yasmine Beale-Rivaya (Texas State University) is an Associate Professor at Texas State University. Her research and publications center on the languages used in borderland communities, with particular focus on the Mozarabs and Mudéjars, living between the Andalusí and Christian frontier from the ninth to the early fourteenth century.

Jason Busic is Associate Professor at Denison University. His research focuses on the intersection between language, religion, and culture in the formation of identity, particularly in the medieval Mediterranean and early modern Iberia. His publications explore this theme in Christian-Muslim encounters and dialogue as communicated in catechism, and apology in medieval and early modern Iberia. Most recently Jason has been focusing on religious-cultural identities as negotiated in the manuscript tradition of Arabic New Testaments of Iberia.

Eva Castro Caridad (Universidad de Santiago de Compostela, Spain): is Professor of Latin in the Department of Classics in the Universidad de Santiago de Compostela, Spain. Dr. Castro-Caridad´s research centers on the Medieval Latin Literature, and on the literary contact between Medieval Hispanic Latin and Medieval Literature. She will be leading the work on the “Materia Clásica” of the GGE.

Robert Daum (Simon Fraser University): is Fellow & Lead, Diversity & Innovation, at Simon Fraser University’s Morris J. Wosk Centre for Dialogue. He earned his PhD in Near Eastern Studies at the University of California, Berkeley, where his dissertation on talmudic literature was completed under the supervision of Daniel Boyarin. His M.A. in Hebrew Literature and Rabbinical degree were earned at HUC-JIR. At the University of British Columbia he is a Faculty Associate of the Institute for Gender, Race, Sexuality and Social Justice, and a Faculty Member of Green College’s Common Room. His published work focuses on late antique and medieval rabbinic traditions, texts and anthologies. An expert in dialogue and public engagement, he is a member of the editorial board of the research and policy journal of the Canadian Race Relations Foundation, and he leads projects for universities and government on equity, diversity and inclusion.

Derek Carr (UBC Vancouver): is Associate Professor Emeritus at UBC Vancouver. Dr. Carr graduated in Spanish and Latin American Studies at the University of Newcastle in the UK and obtained his PhD in Romance Studies at UBC. In addition to his strictly academic preparation, Dr. Carr is also a certified translator, accredited by the Council for Translators and Interpreters of Canada (CTIC), and a founding member of the Society of Translators and Interpreters of British Columbia (STIBC).

Constance Crompton (U Ottawa): Crompton is an assistant professor in the Department of Communication at the University of Ottawa, and Canada Research Chair in Digital Humanities. In addition to her work with the General Estoria team, she is co-director, with Michelle Schwartz, of the Lesbian and Gay Liberation in Canada project, and a researcher with Implementing New Knowledge Environments (INKE). She also serves as the VP English of the Canadian Society for Digital Humanities/Société canadienne des humanités numériques, associate director of the Digital Humanities Summer Institute at the University of Victoria, and as a research collaborator with The Yellow Nineties Online housed at Ryerson University’s Centre for Digital Humanities. Her work has appeared in a number of edited collections and the Victorian Review, Nineteenth-Century Gender Studies, Digital Humanities Quarterly, the UBC Law Review, and Digital Studies/Champs Numerique. She is co-editor, with Richard Lane and Ray Siemens, of Doing Digital Humanities: Practice, Training, Research (Routledge 2016).

Fernando García Andreva (Universidad de la Rioja, Spain) is Assistant Professor at the University of La Rioja, Spain. His research is oriented particularly towards historical linguistics of the Spanish language, with a special emphasis on the study of medieval lexicon and Digital Humanities. His work as a collaborator in the research project “Confluence of Religious Cultures in Medieval Spanish Historiography: A Digital Humanities Project” centers both on the digital edition of the GGE and on the supervision of the critical edition and linguistic analysis.

Claudio García Turza (CILENGUA) is Professor of Spanish Linguistics and Director of the research institute CILENGUA in Spain. He is one of the most renowned Hispanists in the world today and creator and director of the Biblias Hispánicas project. His research focuses especially on Spanish dialectology, historical linguistics of the Spanish language, and the study of the Hispanic Bibles.

David D. Navarro is Associate Professor earned his B.A. and M.A. in English Studies from University of Valladolid (Spain), and University College Dublin (Republic of Ireland). He holds an M.A. and Ph.D. in Hispanic Studies from Western University (London, Ontario, Canada). Dr. Navarro’s research interests include Medieval Iberian Literature and History; Religious Minorities of the Iberian Peninsula; Biblical Exegesis; Jewish-Christian relations; Judeo-Spanish, and Sephardic Diaspora. His scholarly agenda also focuses on Medieval Jurisprudence where is currently working in collaboration with Dr. Yolanda Iglesias of University of Toronto (Canada) on Alphonso X of Castile’s legal compendium ‘Siete Partidas’ (Seven-Part Code).

Ricardo Pichel (Universidad de Alcalá de Henares / Universidade de Santiago de Compostela, Spain) is Postdoctoral Researcher (Juan de la Cierva) at the Universidad de Alcalá and also at the Institute of Galician Studies (Universidade de Santiago de Compostela). His earned his PhD in Galician Philology in the Universidade de Santiago de Compostela with the edition and study of the Historia troyana of Pedro I (extraordinary doctorate award, 2013). He belongs to several research groups (PhiloBiblon- BITAGAP, Petrista Network, FILGA, REMI, SMELPS, GHITE, CHARTA) and he is a research associate in several projects about medieval Iberian studies (The Estoria de España digital project, 7PartidasDigital, Study & Edition of Crónica de 1344, corpus CHARTA, COTAGAL, etc.). He blogs on his current research at: https://ricardopichel.academia.edu/

Joaquín Porras Ordieres (HfJS Heidelberg): is a graduate in History (B.A), Hebrew Philology (B.A), and Religious Sciences (M.A) from the Universidad Complutense de Madrid. As well as in Jewish Civilizations (M.A) from the Hochschule für Jüdische Studien Heidelberg with a thesis on ritual murder and blood libel accusations in Medieval Iberia. He focuses his research on the social history of Jews in Medieval Iberia and its impact on Jewish-Christian relations and anti-Jewish sentiments. In preparation for his PhD dissertation, he is currently studying a trove of 14th-century documents from a northern Castilian village in the border with Navarre and Aragon. He serves as a research assistant in the Neue Gallia Germania Judaica.
